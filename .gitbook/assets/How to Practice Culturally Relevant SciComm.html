How to Practice Culturally Relevant SciComm

Culturally Relevant SciComm

Hola! In this course, learn principles and strategies for culturally relevant science communication, to help you connect science with your audiences lives.

Think about what makes you feel connected to science. What makes science meaningful to you?

For me, it is combustion cooking pinchos or skewers, some of my favorite Puerto Rican street food.

Maybe it was curiosity, an experience from early life, or something about your culture. Most likely, you were able to find a connection with something that fostered your interest in science.

Thats because our culture who we are, our experiences and our background influences our interests in and approaches to science.

For example, I was born and raised in a beach town, on a tropical island in Puerto Rico.

I grew up on a farm, in a rural community where raising animals, growing your food and using medicinal plants was a way of life for many.

I didnt realize it at the time, but my first exposure to science was through that environment. That's why I feel connected to science by the beach, by animals and by people I grew up with.

Culture includes the languages, customs, beliefs, knowledge and identities of an individual or a group of people. All of this shapes how they understand and make sense of the world.

This is why, when communicating science, it's important that we connect it to our audiences culture. People value, understand and engage with science through their own cultural lenses.

Making science relevant to your audiences culture is a great way to make it more pertinent, understandable and accessible.

In other words, culturally relevant science communication helps people make sense of science because it connects it to their everyday lives, who they are and what they care about.

Quick Recap

  • Culture includes the languages, customs, beliefs, knowledge and identities of an individual or a group of people.
  • People make sense of the world through cultural lenses.
  • Culturally relevant science communication makes science more meaningful and pertinent to an audience, because it connects it to their life.

Sometimes when people think about culture, they think only of certain racial or ethnic groups. But the reality is that EVERYONE has culture. No one is neutral or "culture-free."

Its just that the dominant culture in science and science communication is Western and white. Non-Western non-white cultures and people have been historically marginalized from and overlooked by science and science communication.

The dominance of Western, white cultures contributes to exclusion of those who dont belong or conform to Western, white cultural norms.

Communicating science in culturally relevant ways can help counter this marginalization and advance representation and inclusion.

But countering deeply ingrained stereotypes and behaviors requires reflexivity. Reflexivity involves constantly examining your positionality, biases and motivations as well as those of your audience. This reflexivity can then be turned into action.

Turn reflexivity into action: Check your assumptions, recognize what you dont know, practice humility and educate yourself. You can use your privileges to visibilize marginalized individuals, their culture and perspectives.

You might be wondering; how do I make my science communication culturally relevant? Continue to learn more about some important principles and strategies.

1. Consider your audiences context or where they live. Even if youre explaining a universal concept like seed dispersal and how the shape of seeds can help them travel, local examples matter.

For people in Northeastern USA, the mini-helicopter-shaped maple tree seed pod is a relevant example of seed dispersal. But for people in tropical regions, its the African tulip tree seed.

The African tulip tree (meato in my homeland) has seeds surrounded by a thin filmy wing that live by the hundreds in long canoe-shaped pods. When pods open, the seeds float in the tropical air.

2. Consider your audiences identities: racial, ethnic, political, religious, gender and sexual identities, socioeconomic status, and disabilities, among others.

Having a role model with similar identities or lived experiences can help people from historically marginalized groups see themselves succeeding in science.

Its important to remember that one person has intersectional identities, and that people can be marginalized because of different identities at once. For example, Black women and other minoritized women can simultaneously experience sexism and racism, something that is called the double bind.

3. Communicate science in their language(s). Communicating science in a persons first language makes it more accessible and understandable, and thus, more relevant to their lives.

When Corey Gray was at LIGO, he collaborated with his mother, Sharon Yellowfly, to translate the historic discovery of gravitational waves into the endangered language of Blackfoot.

In addition to language, pay attention to words. For example, avoid using jargon! Try using everyday words or expressions that people will be familiar with instead.

4. Think about your audiences values, or the ideals, beliefs and principles they use to guide their actions. We use our values to determine what is correct, desirable or moral.

When discussing topics like climate change, find common ground in values, shared beliefs (like religion) or interests (like hobbies). This helps people who disagree get past their differences.

5. Connect science to your audiences previous knowledge and life experiences, with analogies, metaphors or pop cultural references. You could reference popular songs!

Try to ground science in the culture and context of your audience. Ecologist Danielle Lee uses the song O. P. P. by hip-hop trio Naughty by Nature to teach about a songbird mating behavior!

But just as you consider the culture of your audience, you should consider how your own culture influences the way you understand and communicate science.

Be aware of your own cultural lenses, biases and privileges. Consider how these might impact how you communicate science and how your audience might perceive you. This will help you to be more aware of prejudices you may have and be more open-minded about other perspectives. Use reflexivity!

I worked with the Deaf community in Puerto Rico through the project Aqu Nos Cuidamos. I learned that due to systemic educational barriers and discrimination, some Deaf people lack the proficiency necessary to read captions.

My team and I quickly recognized there was a lot we didnt know. We sought opportunities to educate ourselves and found collaborators in the Deaf community.

As a hearing person, I used to think adding subtitles to videos was enough. But I learned the best way to make content accessible for people who are Deaf is with sign language interpreters.

Quick Recap

To make your science communication culturally relevant:

  • Consider different elements of your audiences culture.
  • Remember, your culture influences how you communicate science, too. Be aware of your biases and privileges.
  • If engaging with a community that is not your own, be humble, educate yourself and partner with members or allies of that community to build connections and trust.

Summary

There are many ways to make science culturally relevant choose the strategies that will help you connect with your audience, meet your objectives and deliver your message most effectively. But remember, culturally relevant science communication requires collaboration, recognition of inequities and reflexicity.
